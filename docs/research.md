# Breathwork Practices (2015–2025): Physiological and Psychological Effects in Healthy Adults and Clinical Populations

## Executive Summary
Breathwork practices have gained empirical support over the past decade as simple, non‑pharmacological tools to improve autonomic regulation and stress resilience. Across techniques, controlled breathing generally enhances parasympathetic activity (often evidenced by increased heart rate variability, HRV) and reduces markers of stress and anxiety. Slow‑paced breathing at ~0.1 Hz (≈6 breaths/min) is consistently associated with improved vagal tone, baroreflex function, and subjective calm. Emerging research suggests that emphasizing prolonged exhalation (e.g., “cyclic sighing”) can yield strong benefits for mood and reductions in physiological arousal. Most practices are low‑risk and show positive effects in both healthy individuals and in populations with conditions like asthma, hypertension, and anxiety disorders. Evidence quality varies by protocol and outcome: while multiple randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and meta‑analyses demonstrate benefits, some studies report modest changes or no added benefit over control conditions, highlighting the need for further high‑quality research and standardized protocols.

## Healthy Adults

### Slow‑Paced Breathing (Resonance ~0.1 Hz)
**Protocol (cadence, duration, frequency studied):** Typically 4–6 breaths/min (often targeting individual resonance ≈6 bpm), 5–20 min/session; sometimes daily over several weeks; may use HRV biofeedback to find personal resonance.

**Outcomes:** Increases HRV (time‑ and frequency‑domain indices), strengthens baroreflex, lowers resting heart rate and blood pressure; subjective relaxation and reduced state anxiety; some studies report improvements in attention/executive function.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Systematic reviews and controlled trials support HRV and mood effects. Example: RCTs in young/middle‑aged adults show increased HRV (e.g., SDNN, total power) and lower perceived stress after 4 weeks of daily resonance breathing; acute 0.1 Hz breathing reduces blood pressure and increases baroreflex sensitivity. Overall, slow breathing has among the strongest evidence in healthy cohorts (multiple RCTs and syntheses).

**Limitations & gaps:** Protocol heterogeneity (ratios, depth, posture); sample sizes often modest; mixed findings on specific HRV bands; durability beyond the training window needs more study.

---

### Box Breathing (4‑4‑4‑4)
**Protocol:** Equal‑length inhale–hold–exhale–hold (commonly 4 s each; ≈4 bpm), 5–10 min sessions, seated.

**Outcomes:** Lower respiratory rate and heart rate; subjective reductions in stress and improved focus; likely modest HRV increases analogous to other paced breathing.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Direct RCT evidence isolating box breathing is limited. A 1‑month RCT comparing three breathing styles and mindfulness found all breathing conditions (including box) improved mood and reduced anxiety, with cyclic sighing outperforming others for some outcomes; HRV changes were not significantly different between groups over that short duration. Much applied use derives from military/first‑responder contexts (observational/expert opinion).

**Limitations & gaps:** Few trials isolate box breathing; optimal counts (4 vs 5–6 s) not standardized; need acute‑stress and longitudinal clinical RCTs.

---

### Cyclic Sighing (Prolonged Exhalation)
**Protocol:** Repeated “physiological sighs”: nasal inhale, brief second top‑off inhale, then extended mouth exhale; practiced continuously ~5 min/day.

**Outcomes:** Rapid reduction in respiratory rate and physiological arousal; increased positive affect and reduced state anxiety; strong parasympathetic engagement via prolonged exhalation.

**Evidence strength + study details:** A high‑profile 1‑month RCT in healthy adults found cyclic sighing produced the largest gains in positive mood and the greatest drop in resting respiratory rate compared with box breathing, a hyperventilation‑style protocol, and mindfulness. Group differences in HRV were not significant over 1 month.

**Limitations & gaps:** Limited to short‑term RCTs; optimal dosing beyond 5 min/day unknown; few physiological endpoints (e.g., cortisol, detailed HRV) reported in head‑to‑head trials.

---

### Alternate‑Nostril Breathing (ANB; Anulom Vilom)
**Protocol:** Slow breathing with alternating nostril inhalation/exhalation; often 4–8 s per phase; sessions 5–20 min.

**Outcomes:** Lower heart rate and blood pressure; increased HRV; improved pulmonary function; some studies show cognitive benefits (attention/memory) and subjective calm.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Systematic review of RCTs reports autonomic and cardiopulmonary benefits. RCTs in young adults show within‑group improvements in pulse/BP and lung function over 4 weeks; immediate HRV increases observed after single sessions at ~6 bpm.

**Limitations & gaps:** Many studies from yoga literature with methodological variability; mechanisms (beyond generalized slow breathing) not fully isolated; long‑term durability unclear.

---

### Diaphragmatic Breathing (DB)
**Protocol:** Slow, deep abdominal breathing engaging the diaphragm; typically 6–10 bpm; sessions 5–20 min; sometimes biofeedback‑guided; multi‑week training.

**Outcomes:** Increased tidal volume and oxygenation efficiency; reduced heart rate and blood pressure; increased HF‑HRV; reduced cortisol; improved attention and negative affect.

**Evidence strength + study details:** RCTs (e.g., 8‑week DB training) show reductions in negative affect, improved sustained attention, and decreased salivary cortisol versus controls. Narrative and quantitative syntheses support vagal/HRV improvements with slow diaphragmatic, nasal breathing.

**Limitations & gaps:** Optimal training “dose” not well defined; some cognitive findings need replication; adherence and coaching quality affect outcomes.

---

### Wim Hof Method (WHM; breathing component)
**Protocol:** Cycles of ~30 fast deep breaths (hyperventilation) followed by prolonged breath retention; 3–4 rounds (~15–20 min). Often paired with cold exposure in full WHM.

**Outcomes:** Acute sympathetic surge (epinephrine), downstream anti‑inflammatory effects (elevated IL‑10, reduced pro‑inflammatory cytokines) in experimental settings; subjective energy/euphoria; mixed findings on HRV at rest; potential improvements in stress tolerance.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Controlled experiments (including endotoxin challenge) show blunted inflammatory responses in WHM‑trained participants mediated by epinephrine/IL‑10 dynamics. Systematic reviews conclude WHM induces anti‑inflammatory effects; effects on performance are mixed.

**Limitations & gaps:** Multimodal method (breath + cold + mindset) complicates attribution; small samples; safety considerations (light‑headedness, syncope risk). More RCTs parsing breathing‑only vs cold‑only effects are needed.

## Clinical Populations

### Slow‑Paced Breathing (Clinical)
**Applications:** Anxiety/panic disorders, PTSD, hypertension, heart failure, chronic pain, insomnia.

**Outcomes:** Reduced anxiety and perceived stress; increased HRV; modest reductions in blood pressure; improved baroreflex sensitivity; better sleep latency/quality in some studies.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Systematic reviews of clinical trials report high proportion of effective interventions for stress/anxiety when sessions are ≥5 min, guided, and practiced repeatedly. Meta‑analyses in hypertension show modest but significant BP reductions (≈3–8 mmHg) and lower heart rate with daily breathing practice; device‑guided slow breathing recognized by guidelines as an effective adjunct.

**Limitations & gaps:** Adherence outside supervision; some null findings with brief/unguided protocols; typically adjunctive, not standalone; need longer follow‑ups and standardized dosing.

---

### Box/Patterned Breathing (Clinical)
**Applications:** Acute anxiety/stress management (pre‑operative settings, emergency/military contexts); grounding in panic/PTSD.

**Outcomes:** Lower pre‑op anxiety and cortisol in small trials; subjective calming and stabilization of breathing.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Limited controlled clinical data; supportive evidence from applied settings and small controlled studies; often included within broader breathing/mindfulness programs rather than isolated.

**Limitations & gaps:** Few RCTs isolating box breathing; optimal parameters and condition‑specific efficacy undetermined.

---

### Cyclic Sighing (Clinical)
**Applications:** Panic disorder, generalized anxiety, PTSD (targeting hyperarousal); potential use for acute regulation.

**Outcomes:** Rapid arousal reduction and subjective calming; hypothesized benefits for hyperventilation‑prone conditions.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Clinical RCTs are pending; early pilot work and case applications suggest feasibility and benefit; strong support in healthy‑adult RCT for mood/arousal.

**Limitations & gaps:** No large published clinical RCTs yet; patient comfort with double‑inhale varies; monitor in those with cardiovascular instability.

---

### Alternate‑Nostril Breathing (Clinical)
**Applications:** Hypertension, asthma/respiratory disorders, anxiety/depression adjunctive care.

**Outcomes:** BP reductions of a few mmHg in small RCTs; improved HRV and pulmonary function; anxiety reduction in small trials.

**Evidence strength + study details:** RCTs/controlled studies suggest beneficial autonomic and respiratory effects; often part of multimodal yoga therapy.

**Limitations & gaps:** Effects often confounded by concurrent practices; mechanisms vs simple slow breathing unclear; adherence issues with nasal obstruction.

---

### Diaphragmatic Breathing (Clinical)
**Applications:** COPD/asthma rehabilitation, anxiety/PTSD, IBS and chronic pain, peri‑operative recovery.

**Outcomes:** Improved breathing efficiency and dyspnea in subsets of COPD; reduced hyperventilation symptoms and improved asthma control; PTSD symptom reductions in pilot RCTs; decreased cortisol/inflammation in depression pilot work; improved IBS symptoms and anxiety in small trials.

**Evidence strength + study details:** Mixed‑quality evidence across conditions; several RCTs and systematic reviews support DB as an adjunct (stronger for asthma/anxiety, emerging for depression/PTSD).

**Limitations & gaps:** Disease‑severity dependent responses; need standardized training and adherence monitoring; blinding impossible, so expectancy may affect self‑report outcomes.

---

### Wim Hof Method (Clinical)
**Applications:** Inflammatory/autoimmune conditions; depression/anxiety; stress resilience.

**Outcomes:** Anti‑inflammatory immune modulation in experimental paradigms; in depression/anxiety, meaningful symptom reductions comparable to slow breathing, with a possible added benefit for rumination.

**Evidence strength + study details:** A 3‑week RCT in women with high depressive symptoms found WHM and an active control (slow breathing + warm showers) both reduced depression (~24%), anxiety (~27%), and perceived stress (~20%) immediately and at 3‑month follow‑up; WHM uniquely reduced daily rumination. Systematic reviews highlight consistent anti‑inflammatory effects but mixed performance outcomes.

**Limitations & gaps:** Higher attrition/acceptability issues vs simpler breathing; safety screening needed; need trials parsing components and testing disease‑specific endpoints.

---

## Limitations & Gaps in the Literature
Protocol heterogeneity, small samples, and short follow‑up are common. Blinding is inherently challenging; active‑control designs help but are not universal. Mechanistic understanding (neural circuits, gas dynamics individualized by CO₂ sensitivity) is improving but incomplete. Head‑to‑head comparisons between techniques are rare (with recent exceptions). Reporting of effect sizes and clinical significance should be more consistent. Overall, breathwork is best viewed as a low‑risk adjunct that improves autonomic balance and stress regulation, with moderate evidence across several outcomes and populations.
